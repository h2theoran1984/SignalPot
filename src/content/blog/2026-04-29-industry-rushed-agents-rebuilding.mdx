[Monte Carlo's "Agents in Production: The Builder's Perspective"](https://www.globenewswire.com/news-release/2026/04/28/3282734/0/en/two-thirds-64-of-enterprise-leaders-and-engineers-rushed-ai-agents-before-they-were-ready-and-are-now-paying-the-price.html), released yesterday, surveyed 260 technology practitioners and engineering leaders at organizations with 1,000 or more employees. The headline number has been picked up everywhere: 64% of respondents say their organization deployed AI agents before feeling fully prepared.

The number underneath it is the one that matters more.

Fifty-three percent of enterprises already expect to significantly rebuild or redesign AI agent systems they have already deployed. Not someday. Already. These are systems that were evaluated, approved, shipped, and are now being slated for fundamental rework — not patches, not configuration changes, but architectural rebuilds of things that cost between $150,000 and $800,000 per implementation to stand up in the first place.

This is what the verification skip tax looks like when it comes due.

## Why Teams Rushed

The 64% stat is not a story about recklessness. It is a story about structural pressure operating on rational people.

Every major cloud provider spent the last eighteen months positioning AI agents as the next layer of enterprise infrastructure. Microsoft made multi-agent orchestration generally available on April 1. Amazon Bedrock AgentCore hit 2 million SDK downloads in five months. Google reported [KPMG deploying over 100 agents in the first month](https://techresearchonline.com/news/google-cloud-next-2026-enterprise-ai-agents/) of Gemini Enterprise adoption. The competitive signal was clear: move now or fall behind.

Executives responded by setting deployment timelines. The [Monte Carlo report](https://www.globenewswire.com/news-release/2026/04/28/3282734/0/en/two-thirds-64-of-enterprise-leaders-and-engineers-rushed-ai-agents-before-they-were-ready-and-are-now-paying-the-price.html) documents exactly what happens next: among the engineers and developers most responsible for keeping those agents running, the figure who say they weren't ready climbs to 75%. The people setting timelines and the people executing against them had different assessments of readiness, and the timeline won.

That gap — between executive confidence and engineering confidence — is not new in software. What is new is the cost of the correction. You can refactor application code in a few sprints. Rebuilding an AI agent system means re-evaluating model behavior, rewriting prompts, fixing integration assumptions that have been propagating downstream for months, and retraining organizational workflows that were built around the original system's behavior. The rebuild isn't just technical — it's organizational.

## The Rollback Problem

The report surfaces a specific failure mode that hasn't gotten enough attention.

Nearly one-third of organizations say they could not disable or roll back a harmful AI agent within minutes. Fourteen percent say they could not do it at all.

That is not a governance gap. It is a fundamental characteristic of how these systems were deployed. Agents that run on schedules, write to shared systems, and trigger downstream workflows don't have a clean off switch because the teams that built them were moving fast enough that they didn't design one in. When something goes wrong — and the [same report's data on rushed deployment suggests it will go wrong](https://www.globenewswire.com/news-release/2026/04/28/3282734/0/en/two-thirds-64-of-enterprise-leaders-and-engineers-rushed-ai-agents-before-they-were-ready-and-are-now-paying-the-price.html) — the damage accrues until someone finds an override at the platform level.

This connects directly to a pattern [we wrote about last week](https://signalpot.dev/blog/multi-agent-surge-kill-switch): Portal26 launched "Agentic Token Controls" specifically to stop runaway agents from burning through budgets before anyone can intervene. A product exists because the kill switch that should have been built in wasn't. The Monte Carlo data is the population-level version of the same story.

## Observability Is Not Enough

Monte Carlo's timing is notable. They released the survey data and, in the same week, launched [Agent Observability](https://www.montecarlodata.com/blog-agent-observability-announcement-features/) — a product that delivers end-to-end visibility across agent context, performance, behavior, and outputs.

The product is real and it addresses a genuine gap. If you have agents running in production and you cannot see what they are doing, you are flying blind. Observability gives you the instrument panel.

But the instrument panel tells you what broke after it broke. The 53% facing rebuilds didn't deploy without observability and then discover problems they couldn't see coming. They deployed without knowing whether their agents were reliable enough to operate in production, and then discovered in production that they weren't. Observability made the failure visible. It didn't change the structural condition that caused it.

[The Stanford AI Index documented a 37% gap](https://www.beri.net/article/stanford-ai-index-2026-agents-66-percent-success) between benchmark performance and real production performance across enterprise AI deployments. That gap isn't closed by watching agents more carefully after deployment. It's closed by measuring agents against representative tasks — your actual task distribution, your actual integration environment, your actual edge cases — before the deployment decision gets made.

The question that wasn't asked, for 64% of the organizations in Monte Carlo's survey, was: *does this agent actually perform reliably on the work I'm about to give it?* Not "can it demo the task," not "does it pass the vendor's benchmark," but: what is its real failure rate on the class of inputs it will encounter in production?

## What the Rebuild Actually Costs

[Gartner predicted last year](https://www.gartner.com/en/newsroom/press-releases/2025-06-25-gartner-predicts-over-40-percent-of-agentic-ai-projects-will-be-canceled-by-end-of-2027) that over 40% of agentic AI projects will be canceled by end of 2027. Monte Carlo's data suggests the failure mode is more expensive than cancellation: the 53% who expect to rebuild aren't stopping — they're doubling the spend. They'll pay to build it again, having already paid to build it wrong.

The average enterprise AI budget grew from $1.2 million per year in 2024 to $7 million in 2026. A significant fraction of that increase isn't buying new capability. It's buying correction.

There is a version of this that doesn't happen. It requires treating pre-deployment evaluation as a prerequisite rather than an afterthought — not demo success, not vendor benchmarks, but domain-specific performance measurement on the tasks you care about, with the data distributions your agents will actually encounter. The teams that do this know what they're deploying before they deploy it. The teams that skip it learn the same information more slowly and expensively, in production, where the rebuild clock is already running.

The Monte Carlo report is the industry confessing that it skipped the step. The 53% expecting to rebuild are living with what that costs.

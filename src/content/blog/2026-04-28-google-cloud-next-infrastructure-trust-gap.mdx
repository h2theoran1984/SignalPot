Google Cloud Next '26 ran April 22–24 in Las Vegas and delivered [260 announcements](https://blog.google/innovation-and-ai/infrastructure-and-cloud/google-cloud/google-cloud-next-26-recap/). The headline product was the [Gemini Enterprise Agent Platform](https://cloud.google.com/blog/products/ai-machine-learning/introducing-gemini-enterprise-agent-platform) — a unified stack for building, governing, and orchestrating AI agents inside large organizations. It consolidates what was previously scattered across Vertex AI and Google Workspace into a single system with a no-code Agent Designer, long-running background agents running in cloud sandboxes, access to more than 200 models including Gemini 3.1, Claude Opus 4.7, and the open Gemma family, and 8th-generation custom TPUs backing it all.

The message was not subtle. From the keynote to the partner sessions, Google's position was consistent: [agents are the architecture now](https://techresearchonline.com/news/google-cloud-next-2026-enterprise-ai-agents/). Not a feature. Not a pilot program. The architecture.

That message is now vendor consensus. Microsoft shipped Agent 365 and Copilot Studio multi-agent orchestration. Amazon has Bedrock AgentCore at 2 million SDK downloads. [NVIDIA launched its Agent Toolkit](https://nvidianews.nvidia.com/news/ai-agents) with Adobe, Salesforce, SAP, and 14 other enterprise partners — and announced a [direct collaboration with Google Cloud](https://blogs.nvidia.com/blog/google-cloud-agentic-physical-ai-factories/) on agentic infrastructure just days before Cloud Next opened. 75% of Google Cloud customers are now using AI products. 330 of them processed over a trillion tokens each in the past 12 months.

The infrastructure problem is solved. The platform war produced winners, and all of them won at the same time.

## The Other Number

Alongside the Cloud Next press releases, there's a parallel dataset that didn't make it onto any keynote slide.

[A March 2026 survey of 650 enterprise technology leaders](https://www.digitalapplied.com/blog/ai-agent-scaling-gap-march-2026-pilot-to-production) found that 78% of enterprises have agent pilots running, and 14% have reached production scale. That means for every seven organizations at Cloud Next nodding along to "agents are the architecture," one has actually gotten there.

[The Stanford AI Index 2026](https://www.beri.net/article/stanford-ai-index-2026-agents-66-percent-success) puts the production rate at 11%: 89% of enterprise AI agents never reach production deployment. [Deloitte's 2026 State of AI research](https://www.pertamapartners.com/insights/ai-project-failure-statistics-2026) found that 42% of companies abandoned at least one AI initiative in 2025, with an average sunk cost per abandoned initiative of $7.2 million. [Gartner's forecast](https://www.gartner.com/en/newsroom/press-releases/2025-06-25-gartner-predicts-over-40-of-agentic-ai-projects-will-be-canceled-by-end-of-2027) is that over 40% of agentic AI projects will be canceled by end of 2027.

These numbers weren't generated by organizations that couldn't build an agent. They were generated by organizations that built agents, deployed them into production environments, and discovered that the agents didn't actually perform reliably enough to justify the investment. The infrastructure was there. The agent wasn't.

## What 260 Announcements Actually Solved

The Gemini Enterprise Agent Platform is serious infrastructure. The no-code Agent Designer means a business analyst can go from idea to deployed agent in hours rather than weeks. The background agent capability means agents can handle long-running workflows without holding a live session open. The 200-plus model catalog means teams aren't locked into a single inference provider.

That's genuinely useful. And it solves a specific, real problem: the cost and friction of building and deploying agents. That problem is now solved. You can stand up an enterprise AI agent with no engineering support in a regulated cloud environment faster than you could provision a VM five years ago.

Here's what it doesn't solve.

The Agent Designer can't tell you whether the agent it produces is actually reliable at its intended task. The cloud sandbox can run your background agent at scale — it doesn't tell you what percentage of its outputs are correct. The 200-model catalog gives you access to every major model — it doesn't benchmark them against your specific use case and data distribution.

Building an agent is no longer the hard part. Knowing whether the agent you built actually works is.

## What Breaks When Deployment Gets Easy

There's a structural problem that no-code deployment makes worse rather than better.

When standing up an agent required an engineering team, the teams shipping agents had high organizational investment in the outcome. They tested more carefully. Senior engineers understood failure modes and pushed back on requests to ship agents that hadn't been adequately evaluated. The barrier to deployment was also, imperfectly, a barrier to deploying untested agents.

No-code tooling removes that friction in both directions. A business analyst can build an agent that takes real actions on real systems — filing tickets, updating CRM records, sending outbound communications — in an afternoon, without ever measuring whether the agent's outputs are reliable under the actual distribution of inputs it will encounter.

The pilot-to-production failure rate isn't caused by infrastructure friction. The [five factors that account for 89% of scaling failures](https://www.beri.net/article/stanford-ai-index-2026-agents-66-percent-success) are integration complexity, inconsistent output quality at volume, absence of monitoring tooling, unclear organizational ownership, and insufficient domain training data. Four of those five are evaluation problems. They're what happens when teams deploy agents without first understanding how those agents actually perform on the tasks they'll face.

Infrastructure quality doesn't fix evaluation gaps. It makes it faster to ship agents that have them.

## The Layer That Isn't in the Platform

Every major cloud vendor has now shipped the layer that handles orchestration: how you build, connect, and run agents. What none of them have shipped is the layer that answers the antecedent question: should you connect this agent to that workflow?

That question requires verified performance against representative tasks in your domain, before deployment. Not benchmark scores from the model vendor — those are capability demonstrations under favorable conditions. Not general-purpose accuracy metrics — those don't predict performance on your specific task distribution. Actual task-level evaluation against the inputs your agent will encounter and the standards it will be held to.

This is why the pilot-to-production gap persists despite every infrastructure improvement. When 78% of enterprises are running agent pilots and 14% have gotten to production, the problem is not that deployment is hard. Google just made deployment easier than it has ever been. The problem is that most teams don't know, before they deploy, whether the agent they've built is reliable at the specific work they're asking it to do.

The platform era is over. Google said it at Cloud Next. The evidence agrees. 260 announcements, 75% of enterprise customers engaged, trillion-token workloads running at scale. The infrastructure is there.

What starts now is the era where "we shipped the agent" has to be followed by a question that actually has an answer: does it work?

The headline from OpenAI this week reads like a validation. [Enterprise now accounts for more than 40% of the company's $25 billion annualized revenue](https://finance.yahoo.com/sectors/technology/articles/openai-says-enterprise-ai-already-183912683.html) — and it's on pace to hit parity with consumer by year-end. Goldman Sachs, State Farm, and DoorDash are listed as production customers. The company just closed a $122 billion funding round at an $852 billion valuation. This is no longer a research project with commercial ambitions. Enterprise AI is a working market.

Now read Gartner.

[Gartner predicts that more than 40% of agentic AI projects will be cancelled by the end of 2027](https://www.gartner.com/en/newsroom/press-releases/2025-06-25-gartner-predicts-over-40-percent-of-agentic-ai-projects-will-be-canceled-by-end-of-2027), citing escalating costs, unclear business value, and — critically — inadequate risk controls. Not because AI agents are a scam. Not because the technology doesn't work. Because most enterprise deployments don't have the evaluation infrastructure to tell the difference between an agent that works and one that looks like it works.

Both of those things are true at the same time. That's the thing worth sitting with.

## The Numbers Are Real. So Are the Gaps.

OpenAI's enterprise growth isn't a rounding error. Goldman Sachs running AI agents in production represents a meaningful shift in how a global financial institution manages its operations. State Farm embedding AI into insurance workflows is real operational transformation. These aren't pilots.

But the enterprise AI market extends well beyond OpenAI's direct clients. And outside the handful of top-tier deployments with real infrastructure behind them, [the picture is considerably messier](https://writer.com/blog/enterprise-ai-adoption-2026/). 79% of organizations report facing significant challenges in AI adoption — a double-digit increase from a year ago. An estimated 40% of AI projects won't reach sustained production. Only 21% of companies have a mature model for governing AI agents.

The deployment wave is real. The readiness for what comes after deployment is not.

## The Agent Washing Problem

Here's something Gartner buried in its 40% prediction that deserves more attention: of the thousands of vendors currently marketing themselves as "agentic AI" providers, [Gartner estimates only approximately 130 are genuine](https://www.gartner.com/en/newsroom/press-releases/2025-06-25-gartner-predicts-over-40-percent-of-agentic-ai-projects-will-be-canceled-by-end-of-2027).

That's a significant number of vendors selling something they're not. Not lying exactly — "agent washing" operates in the comfortable gray area where an enhanced chatbot gets rebranded as an autonomous agent, where RPA workflows get dressed up with an LLM front end, where a glorified retrieval pipeline gets presented as multi-step agentic reasoning. The product demos look good. The performance in controlled conditions holds up. The deployment in production — against real organizational data, real edge cases, real adversarial inputs — is a different story.

Enterprise buyers are largely operating blind here. When you can't independently verify that a vendor's agent actually performs as claimed, you're buying the demo. And demos are optimized to impress, not to reflect what happens when the agent meets your actual workflows.

This is the mechanism behind the 40% cancellation rate. Organizations deploy agents they couldn't adequately evaluate. The business value doesn't materialize. The risk controls turn out to be theoretical. The project gets killed.

## What Goldman Sachs Knows That Most Enterprises Don't

The companies at the top of OpenAI's enterprise client list didn't get there by moving fast and hoping for the best. Goldman Sachs has a technology infrastructure and risk management apparatus that evaluates software before deploying it into financial operations. State Farm has compliance requirements that make "it worked in the demo" an unacceptable evaluation standard.

These organizations have implicit verification frameworks even when they don't call them that. They test before they deploy. They monitor after they deploy. They have standards for what performance looks like and accountability structures when those standards aren't met.

Most enterprises don't. They have a budget, an eager vendor, and a tight timeline from the executive asking why their competitors are already using AI agents. The evaluation that should happen before deployment either doesn't happen or gets compressed into a vendor-run proof of concept optimized for a favorable outcome.

The 40% that Gartner expects to be cancelled in the next 18 months aren't companies that didn't believe in AI agents. They're companies that believed in AI agents enough to deploy them without adequate evidence that they'd deliver the promised value.

## The Verification Gap Is a Business Risk Gap

It's tempting to frame this as a technical problem — better benchmarks, more rigorous evaluation criteria, standardized agent performance testing. That's part of it. But the deeper issue is that most organizations haven't built the organizational muscle to evaluate agents as assets they're accountable for.

Financial assets get stress-tested. Infrastructure components get load-tested. Vendors in regulated industries get audited. AI agents — which are increasingly making decisions that affect financial outcomes, customer experiences, and operational processes — often get a demo and a contract.

[Only 21% of organizations have a mature governance model for AI agents](https://www.strata.io/blog/agentic-identity/the-ai-agent-identity-crisis-new-research-reveals-a-governance-gap/). Only 18% are confident their identity systems can handle agent identities effectively. [67% of executives believe their company has already suffered a data leak or breach due to unapproved AI tools](https://www.informatica.com/blogs/cdo-insights-2026-ai-adoption-accelerates-but-trust-and-governance-lag-behind.html). These aren't numbers from companies that ignored AI — they're numbers from companies that deployed it without the governance infrastructure to manage what they deployed.

## The Market Is Bifurcating

The enterprise AI market is splitting in two.

On one side: organizations that built evaluation infrastructure, deployment standards, and governance frameworks before scaling their agent estates. They're extracting real value and building a compounding advantage as their operational data improves agent performance over time. Goldman Sachs and State Farm are in this group not because they spent more, but because they verified more before committing.

On the other side: organizations that deployed first and are now trying to reverse-engineer governance into production systems that were never designed for it. Some of them will course-correct. Some of them are the 40% in Gartner's forecast.

OpenAI's numbers prove that enterprise AI is serious money. Gartner's numbers prove that serious money doesn't protect you from serious failure if you skip the verification step.

The question worth asking before your next agent procurement isn't *"what does this agent do?"* It's *"how do I know it does what it claims?"*
